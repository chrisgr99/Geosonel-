## Section 6 — Sprites

A sprite is an autonomous agent that moves through the scene under its own logic. Like all source kinds, a sprite has a position and up to four behaviour slots (cyclePattern, hasHit, beenHit, onTick). Unlike triggers and curves, a sprite has its own velocity field and integrates motion under physics each simulation step.

Geometry. A sprite has a position (x, y), a velocity (vx, vy), a visible body sized by displayDiameter, and an optional perpendicular cursor. The body is drawn as a directional teardrop rather than a plain disc: a circle of radius displayDiameter/2 (combined with the score's spriteScale) forms the rounded back, and two straight sides tangent to that circle converge to a point ahead of it, giving the sprite a visible nose. The two tangent sides meet at a fixed 90-degree apex, which places the point at distance r·√2 from the centre and the two tangent contact points 45 degrees off the heading on either side, so the rounded back spans the remaining 270-degree arc. The displayDiameter circle (with spriteScale) still defines the bounding circle the simulation uses for canvas-wall collision under the inside-only rule (section 22), and for object-to-object collision the sprite's centerpoint is what other cursors test against; the teardrop is purely visual.

Direction and fill. The teardrop rotates as a whole so its nose points along the sprite's heading. The heading is the direction of the starting velocity (vx, vy); a sprite whose starting velocity is zero points left by default. During playback the nose tracks the live velocity, holding its last non-zero heading through any moment the sprite is momentarily at rest. The body is filled with the colour sampled from the image pixel under the sprite's centerpoint — the same point that is its position and the anchor of its cursor — at full opacity. The fill opacity is a single tunable value; lowering it would let whatever lies beneath the sprite, such as a trigger it is about to reach, show faintly through the body. The outline, meaning the 270-degree back arc and the two nose lines, is stroked in the sprite's own colour at the normal sprite line width: the colour field is the outline colour, while the fill is the live sampled colour.

Cursor. A sprite has cursorL and cursorR extent fields that define a line segment perpendicular to the sprite's heading, with the two extents measured to the sprite's own left and right of that heading. The cursor is visible and active iff at least one extent is non-zero and mute is unchecked. When active, the cursor sweeps through space as the sprite moves and collides with other sources' centerpoints. When the sprite is stationary the cursor persists in its last non-zero motion direction, so the cursor is visually anchored when the sprite slows to a stop; a sprite that has never moved points left by default, matching the body's resting heading.

Motion. Sprites move freely under physics integration; they are not path-constrained, and sweep-along-a-path behaviour belongs to a curve with an extended cursor rather than to a sprite. The maxSpeed field caps integrated velocity. Each sprite has authored x, y, vx, vy, displayDiameter, and maxSpeed fields that define its starting state; live position and velocity are maintained in a separate runtime state the canvas reads for rendering. See section 22 for the integration step and the inside-only wall rule.

Cycle. A sprite's cyclePattern advances on master BPM × beatsPerCycle wall-clock time, the same as a curve's. Unlike a curve where the cursor sweeps a path and pattern events fire at fixed markers along that path, a sprite has no fixed path: pattern events fire at the sprite's current position when their fractional cycle time arrives, so a wandering sprite's audio output tracks wherever the sprite happens to be at each event time. When beatsPerCycle is greater than zero, the sprite's position resets to its authored starting state at the end of each cycle. Because the simulation is deterministic, an image-driven wandering sprite retraces the same path each cycle, turning the wander into a repeatable musical figure. When beatsPerCycle is zero, the sprite never resets and is free to wander indefinitely.

Behaviour slots. A sprite has up to four behaviour slots:

- cyclePattern: a Strudel mini-notation string defining the sprite's musical content. Fires events at their fractional positions as the cycle advances in time.
- hasHit: a JavaScript function that runs when the sprite's cursor strikes another object.
- beenHit: a JavaScript function that runs when another source's cursor strikes this sprite.
- onTick: a JavaScript function that runs every simulation step. The typical use is to read the local image colour at the sprite's position and influence acceleration, giving the sprite an image-derived force field of its own.

Multiple sprites can share an onTick function reference, so a single function authored once shapes the trajectories of every sprite that points to it.

Soft UI convention: six sprites in the default palette, based on experience from GeoSonix that parameter management past six becomes overwhelming. Not a data-model limit.
