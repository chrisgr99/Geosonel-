## Section 19 — Audio and MIDI Output

GeoSonel routes pattern events through one of several audio engines, with the engine choice scoped per score rather than per object. The active engine lives as a top-level field in scene.json, alongside bpm and the harmony framework settings, and surfaces in the property inspector's always-visible global band at the bottom of the form as an engine dropdown. Choosing the engine is the parent decision the rest of the inspector's audio controls depend on: the per-object voice band reshapes to show the active engine's vocabulary (sound and bank dropdowns under superdough, channel and program-number fields under MIDI, synth class and parameters under Tone.js), and the global band exposes the active engine's score-wide controls (reverb and delay character knobs under superdough, mostly empty under MIDI since its destination provides effects, whatever the engine's shared-effects layer is under others). Different scores can use different engines; the choice travels with the score.

The currently supported engines are **superdough** and **MIDI**. Future candidates include **Tone.js**, the **dough** successor to superdough, and **Csound** running in WebAssembly. Each engine is implemented as a module registered in a dispatcher table inside the firing engine; the table's keys are engine names and the values are objects exposing init, dispatch, cleanup, and the list of per-object voice field names the engine consumes. The firing engine's commit walker looks up the active engine's dispatch function and routes each event through it; adding a new engine is registering a new module, with no change to the firing-engine core.

**Superdough** is the Web Audio sampler-and-synth originally built for Strudel and published as a standalone npm package with no dependency on Strudel itself. It carries the tidal-drum-machines library (TR808, TR909, LinnDrum, and similar, accessible through short names like `bd`, `sd`, `hh`, `cp` with bank prefixes), the VCSL orchestral instrument samples, the dough-samples piano, and over a thousand AKWF wavetables, plus subtractive and FM synthesis with selectable waveforms (sine, sawtooth, square, triangle, pink/white/brown noise, crackle) and an effect catalogue covering filters with envelopes, convolution reverb with generated impulse responses, phaser, delay, sidechain ducking, and the Dirt-inheritance effects vowel, crush, coarse, and shape. Per-object voice under superdough is two dropdowns: a pitched-sound chooser (piano and the synth waveforms today, growing as more pitched-sample banks are loaded) and an unpitched-bank chooser (curated subset of the tidal-drum-machines catalogue plus a "(none)" entry). The dropdowns set the s and bank fields on each event by soft injection: the firing engine reads the per-object voice and fills those fields in on outgoing events that don't already specify them, so a pattern that writes `sound("bd").bank("RolandTR808")` explicitly overrides the per-object default. The pitched-sound dropdown applies when an event has no s field (events from `note()` and `n()` patterns); the bank dropdown applies when an event's s field has no underscore (events from `sound()` patterns with raw drum names). The global band under superdough exposes orbit-shared character knobs: reverb size, reverb fade, reverb lowpass, delay time, delay feedback, and related shaping controls.

Per-object voice under superdough also carries a per-instrument amplitude envelope, because superdough's sampled instruments do not damp on their own the way an external MIDI instrument does. Superdough is fire-and-forget: it has no note-off, so a sampled voice plays for the duration the firing engine passes and then runs whatever tail the recording and envelope produce. The dough-samples piano in particular carries several seconds of pedal-down ring baked into the audio, so without an imposed envelope a sequence of piano notes piles overlapping tails into an indistinct slur. The fix is an amplitude envelope inserted on the superdough dispatch path: a short or zero attack (the sampled instruments' own recorded onset already provides the strike), a brief decay, a high sustain level so the note holds at strength for its gated length, and a short release that damps the note when its gated time ends — the envelope's release standing in for the damper of a real instrument or the note-off of a MIDI patch. The model is deliberately "hold full for the note's intended length, then damp," which in ADSR terms is a high sustain level (sustain is a level, not a time) plus a short release time; the hold length itself is the firing engine's per-event gated duration and is not an envelope parameter. The envelope times are absolute seconds on the Web Audio clock, not fractions of a beat, so the damping character stays constant across tempos while the note's hold length tracks tempo — the two come from different places (hold from the firing engine's tempo-aware duration, envelope times from a fixed per-instrument table), so tempo-correct note lengths and tempo-independent damping fall out naturally. Envelope values are stored in a per-instrument table keyed by sound name, seeded uniformly and then tuned per instrument by ear; sounds with no entry get no imposed envelope. A second-order refinement, deferred to its own commit, ties the release onset to the note's articulated end rather than the full step boundary: the gated duration the envelope's offset rides on can be shortened for staccato (the note damps early, leaving space before the next) or lengthened for legato (the note holds and slightly overlaps the next), via Strudel's clip/`.legato()` field, so articulation rides on the duration the firing engine computes rather than on the fixed envelope shape. The envelope is a superdough-path concern only; MIDI's damping is the receiving instrument's responsibility, reached through note-off, and the real General MIDI soundfont instruments (when a soundfont-capable engine path lands) carry their own patch-level damping and would not need this imposed envelope.

**MIDI** drives external synthesisers and DAWs via Web MIDI on browsers that implement it (Chrome, Edge, recent Firefox). Safari lacks Web MIDI support at the time of this revision and falls back to superdough. Per-object voice under MIDI is port (dropdown of available MIDI outputs), channel (1-16), and program number (0-127). Port and channel are essential; program is optional, useful when a score should be self-describing about which patch plays each part rather than relying on the receiving synth's externally-set state. **MPE (MIDI Polyphonic Expression)** is deferred. MPE adds complexity — channel allocation across a 2-16 channel block, per-note channel tracking, note stealing under polyphony pressure, pitch-bend-range setup messages on engine init — that earns its cost only when there is per-note expression data to send. Today's GeoSonel patterns produce discrete note events without per-note expression curves, so MPE would mostly transmit zero-valued continuous controllers; it becomes worth adding when image-driven dynamic signals start producing per-note modulation. The global band under MIDI is mostly empty since the receiving synth provides any effects.

The candidate future engines extend the same architecture without rearchitecting it. **Tone.js** is a mature browser music framework with FM, AM, polyphonic, and sample-based synths plus an extensive effects library; under Tone.js, per-object voice would be a synth class dropdown (Synth, AMSynth, FMSynth, MembraneSynth, MetalSynth, Sampler, others) plus that class's parameters. **Dough** is Felix Roos's successor to superdough, positioned as a more flexible single-AudioWorklet engine; its per-object voice would resemble superdough's. **Csound** running in WebAssembly is a deeper option for composers who want a venerable synthesis language with decades of orchestra repertoire; per-object voice under Csound would be instrument number plus that instrument's parameters. Beyond named engines, anything that can be built as a custom Web Audio node graph can join the registry as a bespoke engine, since the registry's contract is just init / dispatch / cleanup / voice-field-names.

Per-object voice persists per engine across engine switches. The voice subfield on each scene.json object is nested by engine name, so a curve's voice block looks like `voice: { superdough: { sound: "piano", bank: null }, tone: { synth: "FMSynth", harmonicity: 1.5 } }`, with engine subfields present only for engines that object has actually been configured under. The inspector reads only the active engine's subfield and renders its controls; the other subfields persist invisibly. Switching the score's engine to Tone.js, configuring voices under Tone.js, and switching back to superdough later recovers the earlier superdough voice settings exactly. The data model never destructively migrates state between engines.

Engine lifecycle differs between stateless and stateful engines. Stateless engines (superdough, MIDI) need no per-object setup: each event is dispatched fresh through the engine's dispatch function and forgotten. Stateful engines (Tone.js, Csound) require per-object instance setup and teardown — a Tone.js Synth or PolySynth instance lives across many events, holds envelope state and polyphony tracking, and must be cleaned up when the score closes or the object is deleted. The registry's init and cleanup hooks handle this. Engine initialization can be lazy: a heavy engine (Csound-WASM, which downloads and bootstraps a WebAssembly module on first use) loads only when the first score selects it. Lighter engines (superdough, MIDI) initialize eagerly on app start.

Audio timing is scheduled against AudioContext.currentTime with a per-engine lookahead window. Web MIDI fires events at dispatch time, so its window is narrow (30ms, enough to absorb a dropped frame between ticks). Superdough takes absolute audio-context timestamps and schedules events sample-accurately at the timestamp regardless of dispatch jitter, so its window is wider (100ms) and gives the Web Audio scheduler comfortable scheduling headroom. Future engines pick whichever window matches their scheduling semantics. The firing engine selects the active window each tick based on the active engine, with a past-time clamp on superdough events so slightly-late dispatches fire at "now" instead of being silently dropped.

The audioOutput global preference that drives the current MIDI / superdough switch survives, in the multi-engine design, as the default engine for newly created scores — a starting template rather than a live switch. Existing scores without an engine field default to superdough on load. Future engines extend the preference's enum without changing its role.

Future direction. Superdough's authors are exploring single-AudioWorklet implementations of the engine (the **supradough** and **dough** projects) that consolidate its many Web Audio nodes into a single signal-processing thread. These remain experimental as of this revision, but the migration path is well-defined within superdough's own roadmap, so GeoSonel would inherit any stabilised improvement automatically when it lands. A separate dough engine entry in the dispatcher registry is also possible if dough establishes itself as a distinct user-facing engine choice rather than as an internal evolution of superdough.
